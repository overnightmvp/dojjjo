The Feature Prioritization Framework That Built Billion-Dollar Companies

Your customer interviews revealed dozens of problems and feature ideas. Now comes the hard part: deciding what to build first. Most founders try to build everything. Smart founders build the minimum that proves maximum value.

üöÄ The MVP Definition Framework

MVP = Minimum features needed to Validate your core Promise

Your MVP should do ONE thing incredibly well, not 10 things poorly. It should validate your riskiest assumption with the least amount of work.

üìã Real MVP Examples

Airbnb's MVP: Simple website where people could list and book air mattresses in their apartments. No payment processing, no reviews, no fancy search. Just the core transaction.
Dropbox's MVP: A 3-minute demo video showing the core concept. No actual product built. Validated demand before writing code.
Buffer's MVP: Landing page that collected emails and scheduled social media posts manually. No automation, no dashboard, no analytics.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Feature Creep Trap

You'll be tempted to add "just one more feature" to make it "complete." Resist! Every additional feature:

  • Delays your launch by weeks
  • Adds complexity and bugs
  • Dilutes your core value proposition
  • Makes it harder to get user feedback

üí° The One-Feature Rule:

If your MVP solves one problem perfectly, users will forgive missing features. If it solves 10 problems poorly, users will abandon it completely.

Focus on being remarkable at one thing, not mediocre at everything.
